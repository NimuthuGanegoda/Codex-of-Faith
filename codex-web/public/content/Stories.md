# Sacred Stories and Parables

## Buddhism: The Mustard Seed
A woman named Kisa Gotami lost her only child. Distraught, she went to the Buddha. He told her he could help, but only if she brought him a mustard seed from a house where no one had ever died. She went from house to house, but everywhere she went, she found that death had touched every family. She realized that suffering and death are universal, finding peace in the understanding of impermanence.

## Hinduism: The Churning of the Ocean (Samudra Manthan)
The Devas (gods) and Asuras (demons) once churned the cosmic ocean of milk to obtain Amrita, the nectar of immortality. They used Mount Mandara as a churning rod and the serpent Vasuki as a rope. As they churned, many wonders emerged, but so did a lethal poison. Lord Shiva drank the poison to save the universe, turning his throat blue. Eventually, the nectar emerged, symbolizing the struggle between good and evil and the pursuit of spiritual truth.

## Christianity: The Prodigal Son
A father has two sons. The younger son demands his inheritance early, leaves home, and wastes everything on a life of excess. When a famine strikes and he is starving, he returns home, hoping to work as a servant. Instead, his father runs to meet him, embraces him, and throws a great feast. The story illustrates unconditional love, forgiveness, and the joy of redemption.

## Islam: The Man and the Thirsty Dog
A man was walking on a journey and felt very thirsty. He found a well, climbed down, and drank. When he came out, he saw a dog panting and licking the mud because of extreme thirst. The man thought, "This dog is suffering just as I was." He went back down, filled his shoe with water, and held it in his mouth while climbing out. He gave the water to the dog, and God thanked him and forgave his sins. It teaches the importance of compassion for all living beings.

## Judaism: The Golem of Prague
In the 16th century, Rabbi Judah Loew created a giant man out of clay, a Golem, to protect the Jewish people of Prague from persecution. He brought the Golem to life by placing a sacred word in its mouth. The Golem was a powerful defender but lacked a soul. Eventually, it became too powerful and had to be deactivated, serving as a reminder of the responsibilities that come with creation and the need for wisdom.

## Sikhism: The Story of Bhai Lalo
Guru Nanak was invited to a feast by a wealthy, corrupt official named Malik Bhago, but he chose to eat simple food at the house of a poor, honest carpenter named Bhai Lalo. When questioned, Guru Nanak took a piece of bread from each house. From Bhai Lalo's bread, milk dripped out (symbolizing honest labor), while from Malik Bhago's bread, blood dripped out (symbolizing exploitation). It teaches that honest work is sacred.
