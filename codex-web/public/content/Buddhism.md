# Buddhism: The Path of the Awakened One

## The History of the Awakened One
Buddhism began with Siddhartha Gautama, a prince born in the 5th century BCE in what is now Nepal. He lived a life of sheltered luxury until he witnessed the "Four Sights": an old man, a sick man, a corpse, and an ascetic. Realizing that no amount of wealth could protect him from suffering, he abandoned his throne to find a solution. After years of extreme asceticism and then meditation, he attained Enlightenment under a Bodhi tree, becoming the **Buddha**—the "Awakened One."

## The Four Noble Truths
The foundational logic of Buddhism is structured into four truths:
1. **Dukkha (The Truth of Suffering):** Life inevitably involves dissatisfaction, pain, and impermanence.
2. **Samudaya (The Origin of Suffering):** Suffering is caused by *tanha*—craving, attachment, and ignorant desire.
3. **Nirodha (The Cessation of Suffering):** There is a way to end this cycle by letting go of attachment, leading to Nirvana.
4. **Magga (The Path):** The specific method to achieve this is the Eightfold Path.

## The Noble Eightfold Path
This is the roadmap to discipline and spiritual liberation, divided into three pillars:

### Wisdom (Paññā)
* **Right Understanding:** Accepting the Four Noble Truths and the laws of karma.
* **Right Thought:** Resolving to live with selflessness, love, and non-violence.

### Ethical Conduct (Sīla)
* **Right Speech:** Abstaining from lies, gossip, and harsh words.
* **Right Action:** Behaving morally (no killing, stealing, or sexual misconduct).
* **Right Livelihood:** Earning a living in a way that does not harm others.

### Mental Discipline (Samādhi)
* **Right Effort:** Preventing unwholesome states of mind and encouraging good ones.
* **Right Mindfulness:** Being intensely aware of your body, feelings, and thoughts.
* **Right Concentration:** Developing the deep focus required for true meditation.

## The Goal: Nirvana
The ultimate aim of Buddhist practice is **Nirvana**, a state of liberation from the cycle of birth, death, and rebirth (*Samsara*), characterized by the total extinction of suffering and desire.
